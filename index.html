Basics

What was the British Empire?

The British Empire was a global system of colonies, dominions and territories controlled from London ‚Äì for more than 400 years, it connected very different parts of the world under British rule.

Key facts
  • Timeline: from the late 16th century to the late 20th century.
  • At its height, it ruled over about a quarter of the world‚Äôs land area and population.
  • Included territories in the Americas, Africa, Asia, the Middle East and the Pacific.

Expansion

How did the empire grow?

Over several centuries, Britain expanded its influence through exploration, trade, military force and diplomacy ‚Äì often combining economic interests with political control.

Very rough timeline
  • Early phase (1500s‚Äì1700s): first colonies in Ireland and North America; trading companies like the East India Company begin.
  • Asian & Indian focus (1700s‚Äì1800s): Britain gains control over large parts of India and key ports in Asia.
  • ‚ÄúScramble for Africa‚Äù (late 1800s): European powers divide Africa; Britain controls territories from Egypt to South Africa.
  • Decline & decolonisation (after 1945): independence movements rise, many colonies become independent states.

Peak power

‚ÄúThe sun never sets‚Äù

Around 1920, the British Empire reached its greatest size ‚Äì controlling territories on every inhabited continent.

What this meant
  • Territories in Canada, the Caribbean, large parts of Africa and the Pacific.
  • Control of India, Australia, New Zealand and key regions in the Middle East.
  • Strategic routes: Suez Canal, Singapore, Hong Kong, Cape Town and more.
Think: If one empire controls so many routes and resources ‚Äì how does that affect global trade and politics?

Perspectives

What did life in the empire mean?

Experiences were very different ‚Äì depending on whether you were a British settler, a local ruler, a worker, an enslaved person or part of a resistance movement.

Positions of power
  • British officials and settlers usually had more rights and economic advantages.
  • Some local elites cooperated with the empire to keep or increase their power.
  • For some people, the empire offered access to education, trade or new careers.
Perspectives from below
  • Many communities lost land, resources and political influence.
  • Enslavement, forced labour and racial discrimination were part of the system.
  • Resistance and uprisings appeared in many regions, from India to Africa and the Caribbean.
Question: If we only read sources written by British officials ‚Äì whose voices are missing from the story?
Ambivalence

Benefits ‚Äì and at what cost?

The British Empire built infrastructure and spread ideas ‚Äì but these developments often served imperial interests more than local populations.

Often-mentioned ‚Äúbenefits‚Äù
  • Railways, ports and telegraphs connected regions and made trade faster.
  • English spread as a global language, which today helps in communication and business.
  • Legal and parliamentary systems were introduced in some colonies.
High human costs
  • Economic structures often forced colonies to export raw materials and import British goods.
  • Violent suppression of uprisings, unequal rights and long-term social divisions.
  • Racist ideologies were used to justify domination and unequal treatment.
Critical thought: When someone talks about the ‚Äúgood sides‚Äù of the empire ‚Äì always ask: benefits for whom, and who paid the price?
Turning point

World Wars and the end of the empire

The two World Wars weakened Britain economically and politically ‚Äì and at the same time, they strengthened independence movements in the colonies.

After 1918 & 1945
  • Britain depended heavily on soldiers and resources from its colonies.
  • After 1945, Britain was economically exhausted; the USA and USSR became the main superpowers.
  • Anti-colonial movements grew stronger and demanded self-determination.
Key example: India
  • India was often called the ‚Äújewel in the crown‚Äù of the British Empire.
  • After long struggles and campaigns (for example, Gandhi), India became independent in 1947.
  • This independence inspired many other colonies in Asia and Africa.

Legacy

How does the empire still matter today?

Even though the British Empire ended as a formal political structure, its consequences are still visible in borders, politics, language and culture.

Politics & borders
  • Many borders in Africa and the Middle East were drawn during colonial times, often without respecting local realities.
  • These borders can still cause conflicts and tensions today.
  • Institutions in former colonies ‚Äì parliaments, legal systems ‚Äì often follow British models.
Speaker notes

Connect the historical maps to modern news: conflicts, debates about borders and political systems.

Legacy

Language and culture

The empire also left deep cultural traces ‚Äì some people see them as opportunities, others as reminders of domination.

English as a world language
  • English spread globally through empire and later through globalisation.
  • Today it is a key language in business, diplomacy, science and pop culture.
  • This can open doors ‚Äì but it also raises questions about power and which languages are valued.
Cultural influence
  • Sports like cricket and football, legal ideas and school systems spread around the world.
  • At the same time, many indigenous languages and traditions were suppressed.
  • Today, people in many countries are trying to recover and strengthen local cultures.
Memory

How should we remember the British Empire?

In Britain and in former colonies, people still disagree on how to talk about the empire ‚Äì in museums, in schools and in public spaces.

Debates today
  • Some people focus on achievements like trade, technology and global connections.
  • Others highlight violence, racism, slavery and the destruction of cultures.
  • Movements question statues, street names and symbols that celebrate colonial figures.
Question to the class: Should countries publicly apologise or pay reparations for colonial injustices ‚Äì and why (or why not)?

Wrap-up

What should you remember?

The British Empire is not just a story of maps and dates ‚Äì it is a story of power, inequality, resistance and long-lasting consequences.

Three key points
  • The British Empire was the largest empire in history and deeply shaped global politics and economics.
  • Its history combines trade, infrastructure and cooperation with oppression, racism and violence.
  • Debates about the empire today are debates about justice, memory and identity ‚Äì in Britain and around the world.
Final thought

History, responsibility and the future

We cannot change the past ‚Äì but we can decide how we talk about it and what we do with its consequences.
